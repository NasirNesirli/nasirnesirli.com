The Problem with Lab Software: Why Clinical Labs Deserve Better

Clinical laboratories are using software that was designed in the 1990s, and it shows. Here's what's wrong and why it matters for patient care.
November 9, 2025 · 6 min · Nasir Nesirli

Clinical laboratories are using software that was designed in the 1990s, and it shows.

Walk into most clinical microbiology labs today, and you’ll find talented professionals using interfaces that look like they haven’t been updated since Windows 95. They’re navigating through nested menu systems that require memorizing obscure keyboard shortcuts, manually transcribing data between systems that should communicate seamlessly, and spending significant portions of their day fighting with software instead of focusing on patient care.

This isn’t just an aesthetic problem or a minor inconvenience. When laboratory software is poorly designed, it directly impacts both operational efficiency and patient outcomes. Yet the clinical laboratory software market has remained remarkably resistant to modernization, leaving labs trapped with systems that create more problems than they solve.

The User Experience Problem#

The most immediately obvious issue is user experience. Most laboratory information systems (LIMS) feel like they were designed by database administrators for database administrators, not by people who understand laboratory workflows.

Consider a common scenario: a clinical microbiologist needs to review yesterday’s positive blood cultures and check whether follow-up testing has been completed. In a well-designed system, this should take seconds. In reality, it often requires:

  1. Logging into the primary LIMS
  2. Navigating to the specimen search module
  3. Entering complex query parameters
  4. Scrolling through cryptic result codes
  5. Cross-referencing with the QC module to check test validity
  6. Switching to a separate inventory system to verify reagent status
  7. Maybe even checking a third system for instrument maintenance logs

Each step introduces opportunities for error, and the cognitive overhead of managing multiple systems and interfaces reduces the mental energy available for the complex clinical decision-making that actually requires human expertise.

Data Silos and Integration Nightmares#

Laboratory operations involve dozens of interconnected systems: the main LIMS, inventory management, quality control tracking, instrument interfaces, billing systems, and regulatory reporting platforms. In an ideal world, these would work together seamlessly, with data flowing automatically between systems and presenting a unified view of laboratory operations.

Instead, most labs deal with a patchwork of systems that barely communicate with each other. Want to understand whether last month’s reagent quality issues correlate with QC failures? You’ll need to export data from three different systems, manually align timestamps, and build your own analysis in Excel.

This fragmentation doesn’t just waste time—it obscures important patterns that could improve both laboratory operations and patient care. When data is trapped in silos, it becomes nearly impossible to identify trends, optimize workflows, or make data-driven improvements.

The Compliance Burden#

Clinical laboratories operate in a heavily regulated environment, with requirements from CLIA, CAP, FDA, and various state agencies. Compliance is critical for maintaining accreditation and ensuring patient safety, but current software often makes compliance harder, not easier.

Instead of automatically tracking required quality control procedures and flagging potential issues before they become problems, most systems require manual documentation and retrospective analysis. Quality managers spend enormous amounts of time generating reports for inspections, often manually aggregating data that should be automatically available.

The irony is that laboratories generate massive amounts of data that could make compliance both easier and more meaningful. Every test performed, every quality control check, every reagent lot—it’s all tracked. But the data exists in formats and systems that make analysis difficult, turning compliance from an opportunity for continuous improvement into a periodic scramble to satisfy auditors.

Innovation Stagnation#

Perhaps the most frustrating aspect of the current state is how little innovation occurs in laboratory software. The major vendors have captured markets through long-term contracts and integration complexity, creating high switching costs that reduce competitive pressure.

This market dynamic means that software features lag years or decades behind what’s possible with modern technology. While consumer applications routinely incorporate machine learning, real-time collaboration, and intuitive interfaces, laboratory software remains focused on basic data entry and retrieval.

The result is that laboratories—which are fundamentally data-driven organizations—have limited ability to leverage their data for operational improvements or clinical insights. AMR surveillance that could inform clinical practice remains a manual, periodic process. Inventory optimization relies on historical averages instead of predictive modeling. Quality control analysis focuses on basic trending instead of sophisticated pattern recognition that could identify problems early.

The Cost of Poor Software#

These problems aren’t just inconvenient—they have real costs for laboratories, healthcare systems, and patients:

Operational Inefficiency: Staff spend excessive time on administrative tasks that should be automated, reducing the time available for clinical activities that require human expertise.

Error Rates: Complex, unintuitive interfaces increase the likelihood of data entry errors and make it harder to catch mistakes before they affect patient care.

Staff Satisfaction: Talented laboratory professionals become frustrated with tools that make their jobs harder, contributing to turnover and recruitment challenges.

Missed Opportunities: Data that could drive operational improvements and clinical insights remains underutilized because systems make analysis difficult.

Compliance Risk: Manual processes and poor data integration create gaps that could result in regulatory violations.

What Labs Actually Need#

The solution isn’t to add more features to existing systems—it’s to fundamentally rethink how laboratory software should work. Labs need:

Unified Data Model: All laboratory operations—samples, results, inventory, QC, maintenance—should be part of a single, integrated system that makes connections and patterns visible.

Intelligent Automation: Routine tasks like QC trending, inventory reordering, and compliance documentation should happen automatically, freeing staff to focus on exceptions and clinical decision-making.

Modern Interface Design: Laboratory software should feel as intuitive and responsive as the consumer applications that staff use daily.

Built-in Analytics: Instead of requiring manual data export and analysis, systems should provide real-time insights into laboratory operations, quality trends, and performance metrics.

Flexible Reporting: Generating reports for inspections, management, or clinical staff should be straightforward, with customizable templates and automatic data aggregation.

API-First Architecture: Labs should be able to easily integrate new tools, instruments, and services without vendor lock-in or complex custom development.

The Path Forward#

The good news is that the technology to build better laboratory software exists today. Modern web frameworks, cloud infrastructure, and machine learning platforms provide all the tools needed to create systems that are both powerful and user-friendly.

The challenge is market dynamics. Building software for clinical laboratories requires deep domain expertise, significant regulatory knowledge, and the patience to navigate complex sales cycles. It’s easier for vendors to maintain existing systems than to rebuild them from scratch.

But the opportunity is significant. Laboratories that can access modern, well-designed software will gain substantial advantages in efficiency, compliance, and data utilization. As healthcare systems face increasing pressure to improve outcomes while controlling costs, the labs with the best operational tools will be best positioned to contribute to these goals.

This is why projects like Quanta matter. By building modern laboratory software with actual laboratory professionals involved in every design decision, we can demonstrate what becomes possible when software truly serves the needs of its users.

Clinical laboratories deserve software that enhances their capabilities rather than hindering them. The technology exists to build it—now it’s a matter of making it happen.
